My Journey into Dolby vs DTS: From DVDs to Dolby Atmos

Audio Notes

Sep 21, 2025

Back in the COVID time, I thought of setting up a home theater. Two names kept popping up everywhere: Dolby and DTS. Both companies rule the cinema and home theater world, but at that time I had zero clue what the differences were. So I started researching, and here’s what I found — mixed with a little bit of my own experience.

The early days

Dolby’s journey began with Dolby Stereo in the late 70s. By the 90s, we got Dolby Digital (AC-3). This was the first digital lossy surround sound that became standard on DVDs. I still remember our old DVD player proudly showing the Dolby Digital logo. It could do 5.1 surround, usually at bitrates around 384–640 kbps.

DTS had its own equivalent: simply DTS 5.1 Core. It did the same thing but at higher bitrates — 768 kbps up to 1.5 Mbps. Many people said DTS sounded clearer, and technically it was, but I never experienced it back then. Our home setup was stuck with Dolby Digital.

The Blu-ray era

Then came the Blu-ray and the PlayStation 3 generation. Dolby upgraded to Dolby Digital Plus (DD+). This was more efficient than the old Dolby Digital, able to scale up to ~1 Mbps, and even carry extra metadata for things like Atmos later on.

DTS countered with DTS-HD High Resolution, which was their lossy “HD” format. It could go as high as 6 Mbps — much higher than Dolby Digital Plus. Again, in theory it was better, but in practice I never got to hear it.

Lossless sound — TrueHD vs DTS-HD Master

Blu-ray also brought the ultimate tier: Dolby TrueHD, a lossless codec with up to 7.1 channels and bitrates around 18 Mbps. This was studio-master quality, no compression artifacts.

DTS answered with DTS-HD Master Audio, also lossless, running even higher, up to 24.5 Mbps. Both were amazing on paper. If you had the right receiver and speakers, this was the real deal.

Atmos vs DTS:X

By the mid-2010s, surround wasn’t enough anymore. Dolby launched Dolby Atmos in cinemas (2012) and later at home. Instead of just fixed channels, Atmos uses objects that can move in 3D space — over your head, around you, wherever the sound designer wants.

DTS replied with DTS:X in 2015. It’s also object-based and flexible, scaling to whatever speaker layout you have. The problem is content. While Dolby Atmos is everywhere — Blu-ray, Apple TV, Netflix, Disney+ — DTS:X is still rare, and often requires renting or buying discs through special services.

Wrapping up

The main reason I stuck with Dolby is simple: when I heard that Apple Music was going to support Dolby Atmos and Lossless Audio, I was sold. A streaming service, at a cheap price, letting you enjoy music in Atmos — it really gives that magic and butterflies. This was enough for me to fully embrace Dolby in my setup.

Someday, I do want to set up a DTS:X system and experience it myself. I’ve heard from audiophiles and movie lovers that it’s wild and amazing. For now though, it isn’t practical for me. But maybe in the future — fingers crossed — I’ll get the chance. Until then, Dolby has been more than enough, and it hasn’t disappointed me one bit.
